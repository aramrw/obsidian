[[Numerical Linear Algebra]]

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LU_decomposition)

> In [numerical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_analysis "Numerical analysis") and [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"), **lower–upper** (**LU**) **decomposition** or **factorization** factors a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_\(mathematics\) "Matrix (mathematics)") as the product of a lower [triangular matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangular_matrix "Triangular matrix") and an upper triangular matrix (see [matrix multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_multiplication "Matrix multiplication") and [matrix decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_decomposition "Matrix decomposition")). The product sometimes includes a [permutation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation_matrix "Permutation matrix") as well. 
> 
> LU decomposition can be viewed as the matrix form of [Gaussian elimination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_elimination "Gaussian elimination"). Computers usually solve square [systems of linear equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_of_linear_equations "System of linear equations") using LU decomposition, and it is also a key step when inverting a matrix or computing the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of a matrix. It is also sometimes referred to as **LR** decomposition (factors into left and right triangular matrices). 
> 
> The LU decomposition algorithm for general matrices was introduced by the Polish astronomer [Tadeusz Banachiewicz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tadeusz_Banachiewicz "Tadeusz Banachiewicz") in 1938.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LU_decomposition#cite_note-Schwarzenberg-1)
