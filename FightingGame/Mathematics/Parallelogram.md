> In [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), a **parallelogram** is a [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_\(mathematics\) "Chirality (mathematics)") [simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon") (non-[self-intersecting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_self-intersecting_polygons "List of self-intersecting polygons")) [[Quadrilateral]] with two pairs of parallel sides. 
> 
> The opposite or facing sides of a parallelogram are of equal length and the opposite angles of a parallelogram are of equal measure. The [congruence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_\(geometry\) "Congruence (geometry)") of opposite sides and opposite angles is a direct consequence of the Euclidean [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate") and neither condition can be proven without appealing to the Euclidean parallel postulate or one of its equivalent formulations.
> 
> By comparison, a quadrilateral with at least one pair of parallel sides is a [trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid") in American English or a trapezium in British English.
> 
> The three-dimensional counterpart of a parallelogram is a [parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped").
> 
> The word "parallelogram" comes from the Greek παραλληλό-γραμμον, _parallēló-grammon_, which means "a shape of parallel lines".

## Special cases

- [Rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle") – A parallelogram with four right angles.
- [Rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus") – A parallelogram with four sides of equal length.
- [Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_\(geometry\) "Square (geometry)") – A parallelogram with four sides of equal length and four right angles.
- Rhomboid – A parallelogram with adjacent sides that are of unequal lengths and [non-right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle#Types_of_angles "Angle") (and thus is neither a rectangle nor a rhombus). This term is not used in modern mathematics but it does survive in some contexts in biology in names like the [rhomboid muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhomboid_muscles "Rhomboid muscles") or [rhomboid leaf shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_leaf_morphology#Leaf_and_leaflet_shapes "Glossary of leaf morphology").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram#cite_note-1)
