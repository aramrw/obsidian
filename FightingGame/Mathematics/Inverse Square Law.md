# Uses

> The inverse-square law generally applies when some force, energy, or [other conserved quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") is evenly radiated outward from a [point source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_source "Point source") in [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"). 
> 
> The [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area") of a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") (which is 4π_r_2) is proportional to the square ($^2$) of the radius $r$, as the [emitted radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux_density "Flux density") gets farther from the source, it is spread out over an area that is increasing in proportion to the square of the distance from the source. 
> 
> Hence, the intensity of radiation passing through any unit area (directly facing the point source) is inversely proportional to the square of the distance from the point source. [Gauss's law for gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law_for_gravity "Gauss's law for gravity") is similarly applicable, and can be used with any physical quantity that acts in accordance with the inverse-square relationship.

### Formula

In mathematical notation, the inverse-square law can be expressed as an:
- Intensity ($I$) varying as a function of distance ($d$) from some center. 
- The intensity is proportional ($\propto$) to the reciprocal of the square of the distance, thus:

$$I \propto \frac{1}{d^2}$$

It can also be mathematically expressed as a ratio:

$$\frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{d_2^2}{d_1^2}$$

Or as the formulation of a constant quantity:

$$I_1 \times d_1^2 = I_2 \times d_2^2$$

### Divergence and Applications

The divergence of a vector field, which is the resultant of radial inverse-square law fields with respect to one or more sources, is proportional to the strength of the local sources, and hence zero outside sources.

Newton's law of universal gravitation follows an inverse-square law, as do the effects of:

- Electric fields
    
- Light
    
- Sound
    
- Radiation phenomena
    

### Theoretical Limitations

It is necessary to examine the boundaries of this principle rather than accepting it as an unconditional absolute. The inverse-square law operates strictly on the assumption of a perfect, isotropic point source propagating through an ideal non-absorbing vacuum.

In practical conditions, this model is frequently insufficient. Phenomena such as sound and light are subject to absorption, scattering, and reflection by the physical medium through which they travel. Furthermore, for extended spatial sources (such as a large fluorescent panel) or in confined environments where waves bounce, the inverse-square law ceases to be accurate, and alternative mathematical models must be applied to correctly describe the behavior of the field.
