> In [classical cryptography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_cipher "Classical cipher"), the **Hill cipher** is a [polygraphic substitution cipher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygraphic_substitution "Polygraphic substitution") based on [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"). Invented by [Lester S. Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lester_S._Hill "Lester S. Hill") in 1929, it was the first polygraphic cipher in which it was practical (though barely) to operate on more than three symbols at once.

### Encryption

> To encrypt a message, each block of _n_ letters (considered as an _n_-component [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")) is multiplied by an [invertible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertible_matrix "Invertible matrix"), or non-[[Singular Matrix]] _n_ × _n_ [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_\(mathematics\) "Matrix (mathematics)"), against [modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_arithmetic "Modular arithmetic") 26. To decrypt the message, each block is multiplied by the inverse of the matrix used for encryption.
