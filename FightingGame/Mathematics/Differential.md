> In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **differential** refers to several related notions[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_\(mathematics\)#cite_note-1) derived from the early days of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), put on a rigorous footing, such as [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") differences and the [derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of functions.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_\(mathematics\)#cite_note-2)

> The term is used in various branches of mathematics such as [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") and [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology").


The term **differential** is used nonrigorously in [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") to refer to an [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") ("infinitely small") change in some [varying quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_\(mathematics\) "Variable (mathematics)"). 

For example, if _x_ is a [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_\(mathematics\) "Variable (mathematics)"), then a change in the value of _x_ is often denoted Δ_x_ (pronounced [delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delta_\(Greek\) "Delta (Greek)") ). 

The differential _dx_ represents an infinitely small change in the variable _x_. The idea of an infinitely small or infinitely slow change is, intuitively, extremely useful, and there are a number of ways to make the notion mathematically precise.

Using [[Calculus]], it is possible to relate the infinitely small changes of various variables to each other mathematically using [[Derivative]]s. 

If _y_ is a function of _x_, then the differential _dy_ of _y_ is related to _dx_ by the formula  

$$${\displaystyle dy={\frac {dy}{dx}}\,dx,}$$

where  ${\displaystyle {\frac {dy}{dx}}\,}$ denotes not 'dy divided by dx' as one would intuitively read, but 'the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of _y_ with respect to _x_ '. This formula summarizes the idea that the derivative of _y_ with respect to _x_ is the limit of the ratio of differences Δ_y_/Δ_x_ as Δ_x_ approaches zero.
