> In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **derivative** is a fundamental tool that quantifies the sensitivity to change of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_\(mathematics\) "Function (mathematics)")'s output with respect to its input. 
> 
> The derivative of a function of a single variable at a chosen input value, when it exists, is the [slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope "Slope") of the [tangent line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent") to the [graph of the function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") at that point. 
> 
> The tangent line is the best [linear approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_approximation "Linear approximation") of the function near that input value. The derivative is often described as the **instantaneous rate of change**, the ratio of the instantaneous change in the dependent variable to that of the independent variable. The process of finding a derivative is called **differentiation**.

Differentiation strips the function down to its core rate of change; [[Integrals|integration]] accumulates that rate of change back into a total value.

## Examples

To master the [[antiderivative]], the forward operation—the **derivative**—must first be understood.

Differentiation is the mathematical tool used to calculate the **instantaneous rate of change**. While algebra can find the average speed of a journey, calculus finds the exact speed at one precise split-second.

## 1. The Core Concept: From Average to Instantaneous

To understand a derivative, consider the formula for the slope of a straight line:

$$\text{Slope} = \frac{\text{Change in } y}{\text{Change in } x} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$$

On a straight line, the slope is constant. On a curve, however, the slope changes at every single point.

If you want to find the slope of a curve at one exact point, you cannot pick two distinct points to calculate $\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$, because that would give you the _average_ slope between them (a secant line).

Calculus solves this by taking two points, bringing them infinitely close together, and calculating the limit as the distance between them approaches zero. The result is the slope of the **tangent line**—a straight line that grazes the curve at exactly one point. That slope is the **derivative**.

## 2. The Power Rule for Derivatives

Just as integration has a power rule, differentiation has its own, which executes the exact opposite operations in the opposite order.

To find the derivative of a variable raised to an exponent:

1. **Bring the exponent to the front** to multiply by the variable.
    
2. **Subtract 1** from the original exponent.
    

### The General Formula:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = n \cdot x^{n-1}$$

_(The notation $\frac{d}{dx}$ simply means "the derivative with respect to $x$")._

## 3. Step-by-Step Example Problems

---

### Example 1: A Single Power Term

Find the derivative of $x^3$.

- **Step 1:** Identify the exponent, which is $n = 3$.
    
- **Step 2:** Bring the $3$ to the front: $3x$.
    
- **Step 3:** Subtract $1$ from the exponent: $3 - 1 = 2$.
    

$$\frac{d}{dx}[x^3] = 3x^2$$

_Connection:_ Notice that the derivative of $x^3$ is $3x^2$. This is exactly why the antiderivative of $3x^2$ is $x^3$.

### Example 2: Handling Coefficients

Find the derivative of $-2x^2$.

When a term has a number in front, leave it there and multiply it by the exponent you bring down.

- **Step 1:** Identify the exponent, which is $n = 2$.
    
- **Step 2:** Bring the $2$ to the front and multiply it by the existing coefficient ($-2$): $-2 \cdot 2 = -4$.
    
- **Step 3:** Subtract $1$ from the exponent: $2 - 1 = 1$.
    

$$\frac{d}{dx}[-2x^2] = -4x^1 = -4x$$

### Example 3: A Linear Term

Find the derivative of $5x$.

The exponent on $x$ is implicitly $1$ ($5x^1$).

- **Step 1:** Bring the $1$ to the front: $1 \cdot 5 = 5$.
    
- **Step 2:** Subtract $1$ from the exponent: $1 - 1 = 0$.
    
- **Step 3:** Evaluate: $5x^0$. Since any number to the power of $0$ is $1$, this simplifies to $5$.
    

$$\frac{d}{dx}[5x] = 5$$

_Concept:_ A function like $y = 5x$ is a straight line. Its rate of change (slope) is always constantly $5$.

### Example 4: A Constant Term

Find the derivative of a standalone constant, like $+ 10$ or any constant $C$.

A constant value does not change; its graph is a flat, horizontal line.

- Because it has zero rate of change, **the derivative of any constant is always 0**.
    

$$\frac{d}{dx}[10] = 0$$

## 4. Putting It Together: Differentiating a Polynomial

To find the derivative of a full polynomial, differentiate each term completely independently. Let us take the function generated by our antiderivative problem and apply differentiation:

$$F(x) = x^3 - 2x^2 + 5x + 10$$

1. **First Term:** The derivative of $x^3$ becomes **$3x^2$**.
    
2. **Second Term:** The derivative of $-2x^2$ becomes **$-4x$**.
    
3. **Third Term:** The derivative of $5x$ becomes **$5$**.
    
4. **Fourth Term:** The derivative of the constant $10$ becomes **$0$**.
    

Combine the results:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[x^3 - 2x^2 + 5x + 10] = 3x^2 - 4x + 5$$

This returns us exactly to the [[Integrand]] from the very first [[Integrals#Definite Integrals]] problem. Differentiation strips the function down to its core rate of change; integration accumulates that rate of change back into a total value.
