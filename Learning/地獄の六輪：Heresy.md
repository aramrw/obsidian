> **Gospel of Thomas** (44) [Jesus said](https://www.earlychristianwritings.com/thomas/gospelthomas44.html#:~:text=(44)%20Jesus%20said%3A%20He%20who%20blasphemes%20against%20the%20Father%20will%20be%20forgiven%2C%20and%20he%20who%20blasphemes%20against%20the%20Son%20will%20be%20forgiven%3B%20but%20he%20who%20blasphemes%20against%20the%20Holy%20Spirit%20will%20not%20be%20forgiven%2C%20either%20on%20earth%20or%20in%20heaven.): He who blasphemes against the Father will be forgiven, and he who blasphemes against the Son will be forgiven; but he who blasphemes against the Holy Spirit will not be forgiven, either on earth or in heaven. 

> Dante opts for the most generic conception of heresy--**the denial of the soul's immortality** (_Inf._ 10.15)--perhaps in deference to spiritual and philosophical positions of specific characters he wishes to feature here, or perhaps for the opportunity to present an especially effective form of [[Contrapasso]]: heretical souls eternally tormented in fiery tombs. More commonly, heresy in the Middle Ages was a product of acrimonious disputes over Christian doctrine, i**n particular the theologically correct ways of understanding the Trinity and Christ.** Crusades were waged against "heretical sects," (such as the Cathars) and individuals accused of other crimes or sins--e.g., witchcraft, usury, sodomy--were frequently labeled heretics as well.

> 	In this Realm lies the Souls of those who either refused to believe in God or hindered His Works on Earth. Blasphemers and related irreligious are also punished here. In most cases, they did this full knowing of their transgressions. Their punishment are to be secured inside of Tombs and set on fire by Demons. As they burn, well aware of their searing pain – they have no one to turn to to relieve such suffering (neither Lucifer or God will hear them). So they will suffer for all Eternity.
